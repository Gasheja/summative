Renewable Energy

There are several renewable sources of energy. Some of them are discussed below:

Solar Energy

Solar energy is captured from sunlight using photovoltaic (PV) cells in solar panels or solar concentrators. These cells convert sunlight directly into electricity. Solar power is versatile and can be used for small-scale applications, such as powering homes or devices, as well as large-scale solar farms that feed electricity into the grid. Advances in solar technology, such as solar battery storage, have increased its reliability, allowing energy to be stored for use when the sun isn’t shining.
Benefits:
Abundant, widely available, low environmental impact.
Limitations: Requires sunlight, so its effectiveness can be reduced in cloudy areas or during nighttime.

Wind Energy

Wind energy is generated by wind turbines that convert the kinetic energy of wind into mechanical power, which is then converted into electricity. Wind farms can be onshore or offshore, with offshore turbines benefiting from stronger and more consistent winds. Wind power is one of the fastest-growing sources of renewable energy worldwide, particularly in regions with strong wind patterns.
Benefits: Clean, renewable, and cost-effective in the long run.
Limitations: Wind is intermittent, requiring backup systems, and turbines can sometimes impact local wildlife (such as birds).

Hydropower

Hydropower harnesses the energy from flowing water (usually rivers or water released from reservoirs) to generate electricity. The most common form is the use of dams, which store water in a reservoir and release it through turbines to produce power. Smaller-scale "run-of-the-river" projects divert some of a river’s flow to generate electricity without needing large dams.
Benefits: Reliable, consistent, can produce large amounts of power.
Limitations: Large dams can disrupt local ecosystems, fish populations, and human communities, and can be vulnerable to drought.

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy taps into the Earth’s internal heat, found beneath the Earth's surface, to generate electricity or provide heating. This heat can be used directly (as in geothermal heating systems) or can be converted into electricity through geothermal power plants, which pump hot water or steam to drive turbines.
Benefits: Reliable, operates continuously (not dependent on weather conditions), low emissions.
Limitations: Geothermal plants are location-specific, requiring areas with high geothermal activity (e.g., volcanic regions), and there is potential for minor seismic disturbances.
